Adding & Formatting Dialogue Year 7

Quick Rules

  • New paragraph every time a new person speaks.
  • If the same speaker continues, keep it in the same paragraph.
  • Use quotation marks around spoken words.
  • Punctuation goes inside the quotation marks (e.g., commas, full stops, question marks).
  • Use dialogue tags (said, asked, replied) or actions to show who is speaking.
  • Make dialogue develop the story or characters — not filler.

Tip: Read your dialogue aloud. Does it sound like real people speaking?

Formatting Guide — Dialogue in Narratives

Use this as a quick reference while you write. Examples use double quotation marks; if your school prefers single quotation marks, use them consistently.

1) Basic tag at the end

“Let’s go,” said Ava.

2) Tag at the start

Ava said, “Let’s go.”

3) Tag in the middle

“Let’s,” said Ava, “go.”

4) Question / exclamation

“Are you ready?” asked Jay. “Hurry up!” he shouted.

5) Action beats (no “said”)

Ava shrugged. “Maybe later.”

6) Same speaker continues

“It’s late.” Jay rubbed his eyes. “We should head back.”

7) New paragraph = new speaker

“I’m in,” said Ava.
“Great,” replied Jay.

8) Interrupted speech

“Wait—don’t open—” The door slammed.

9) Trailing off

“I thought you said…”

10) Quotation inside dialogue

“Mr Lee said, ‘Hand it in by Friday.’”

Formatting checklist
  • Punctuation sits inside the quotation marks.
  • Dialogue tags like said/asked/replied are lowercase unless they begin a sentence.
  • Capitalize the first word inside the quotes if it starts a new sentence.
  • Use an em dash (—) for interruptions; use ellipses (…) for trailing off.
  • Be consistent with single vs double quotation marks (your school style).
